FOBI is a group project focused on how poverty and inequality affect health. Through outreach visits, interviews, and donations, we aim to support children, patients, and residents who are often overlooked.

Why This Matters

The health impact of poverty and inequality is both urgent and preventable.

Poverty is the lack of financial resources and access to basic needs such as food, shelter, and healthcare. It often results in preventable illness, poor hygiene, untreated mental health struggles, and premature death.

Access to healthcare, education, and social support varies across Nigeria, and some institutions such as orphanages, rehabilitation centers, and mental health facilities face challenges in funding and public perception. According to a 2022 UNICEF report, many vulnerable children and adolescents in Nigeria lack adequate access to essential services, and mental health facilities remain under-resourced and stigmatized.

Our Focus

Compassion in action: health, dignity, connection.

Material Support

Provide food, hygiene supplies, and practical items that immediately improve daily well-being.

Human Attention

Spend intentional time with residents and patients to remind them that they are seen, valued, and respected.

Listening and Learning

Interview staff to understand realities and inform more meaningful conversations about change.

Stigma Reduction

Promote empathy for mental health treatment seekers by engaging with them as individuals with stories and dignity.

Outreach Locations

Places where care, listening, and support can make a real difference.

01

Orphanage Visit

We donated food and hygiene supplies, spent meaningful time with the children, and spoke with staff and residents to better understand the challenges they face. We also toured the facilities to learn about their standard of living and asked questions to gain insight into their quality of life.

02

Rehabilitation Centre

We interviewed staff, toured the facilities, and observed daily activities to gain a deeper understanding of the quality of life and the challenges they face. We also engaged in conversations with both staff and residents to learn about their needs, experiences, and aspirations.

Our Message

This project is a small but meaningful step toward addressing poverty and inequality in health within Nigeria. We hope to provide support, inspire compassion, and start conversations that lead to real change.

Our Observations

What each of us observed and what we believe needs to improve.

Brian Anuakpado

Observed: A lady there drew with her feet and was viral on TikTok. This really showed me that nothing is impossible. The woman in charge said they often receive many people. I really hope more people come to help. I pray everyone in need around the world gets the help they need.

Needs Improvement: More volunteers and donations would make a big difference.

Oyindamola Akanle

Observed:I learned that rehabilitation centers are not places for people who are dangerous, but rather support systems similar to hospitals. External factors, such as negative comments or societal attitudes, can slow down patients’ progress, as noted by a doctor we spoke to. Before this experience, I personally held misconceptions and viewed some individuals as “crazy,” which reflects how widespread this misunderstanding is.

Needs Improvement:Rehabilitation centers should be normalized and viewed the same way as hospitals. Public education needs to challenge harmful stereotypes and language, while promoting empathy and accurate understanding of mental and physical disabilities.

Imara Williams

Observed: Many young people are aware that inequality exists, but they often do not think deeply about it or understand how to respond in real-life situations. When it comes to interacting with individuals with disabilities, people may feel awkward, unsure, or even afraid, not out of bad intentions, but because they have not had enough exposure or meaningful experiences to build understanding and confidence.

Needs Improvement: There needs to be more practical, everyday awareness. Schools and communities should create opportunities for students to engage directly through interaction, volunteering, and shared activities. This kind of exposure can help young people become more comfortable, empathetic, and inclusive, rather than distant or uncertain.
